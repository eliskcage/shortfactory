What is Computanium?

Matter has five known states. Solid, liquid, gas, plasma, Bose-Einstein condensate. Computanium is the sixth — and the first one that thinks.

The core idea: when two information structures are merged, the merge operation itself produces a measurable truth score. That score is not computed — it is the state. The substance doesn't store information. It is the comparison. Like how temperature isn't stored in a thermometer — it's the state of the mercury.

Why it matters physically: existing computing substrates (silicon, photonic, quantum) all separate the memory from the operation. Computanium collapses that separation. The material that holds the data is simultaneously the material performing the merge. This enables computation at densities and speeds that Von Neumann architectures cannot reach.

Where This Goes

The money is in biology.

Silicon AGI is the warm-up act. The real prize is what happens when AGI moves into biological systems — when the intelligence isn't running on chips but on matter that was already alive.

This isn't science fiction. The toolkit exists right now: CRISPR-Cas9 for gene editing, DNA data storage (1 exabyte per gram), synthetic biology for programmable cells, and nanoscale motors that have been running in living organisms for decades. What's missing is the cognitive layer — the AGI that can navigate, decide, and adapt inside a cell without a data centre behind it. Computanium is that layer.
