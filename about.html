The Game
Trump vs Deep State — what it actually is.

A political satire browser game where every tap is a blow against the dying corporate beast. 8 action buttons, a purity meter, a Deep State health bar, threat systems, an emergency slot machine, a full courtroom litigation minigame, crowdsourced video content, unlockable characters including Hitler, and a pipe bomb detonator sequence. All running on vanilla JavaScript in a mobile browser. No frameworks. No React. No build tools. Just 3,500 lines of main.js and a conductor who wouldn't stop until it felt right.

Why it's addictive.
The loop is tight. Tap an action, watch purity climb, watch Deep State HP drain, dodge threats, hit emergencies, survive attacks. Every round the pressure increases. Term 1 scales at difficulty 10, Term 2 jumps to 17. You feel the squeeze.
The slot machine is crack. Emergency events trigger a spinning roulette — Nuke, Litigation, Black Ops, or the Executive Wheel. You don't know what's coming. The roulette sound plays once, stops when it lands. Every spin could save you or end you.
Oil economy. Tap oil, collect barrels, sell at the right price. Prices fluctuate every round — 15% chance of 2x, 20% chance of 0.5x. At 5x multiplier the sell button flashes green and the AI screams "SELL SELL SELL!" in your ear via Web Speech API. You can hear the greed.
Threat meters that fight each other. Muslim threat (poop meter, max 10 poops), feminist threat (heart meter, max 10 rainbow hearts). They're inversely linked — feminism above 50 suppresses Muslim threat, and vice versa. War removes poops. Mean tweets remove hearts. At 6+ on either meter, the screen flashes red warnings. At 10, it goes critical. The player is constantly triage-managing two ideological wars that cancel each other out.
Impeachment as a mechanic. HP drops below 15%? Forced into litigation. "IMPEACHED" slams across the map. An AI voice speaks random legal phrases while you fight a courtroom minigame — 7,535 lines of litigation code. Win and you get 5 grace rounds with HP bumped to 20%. Lose and you're done. It's not a cutscene. It's a survival mechanic.
The phone layout problem (and how we solved it).

Fitting 8 action buttons, a map with video loops, threat meters, a purity bar, HP bar, Deep State bar, advisor portraits, oil economy, news ticker, and emergency overlays onto a phone screen — that's the actual engineering challenge. Not the logic. The layout.

Squash-and-expand. During vote phases, advisor heads compress from 160px to 100px with CSS scale(0.85), the vote map expands from 180px to 250px, then auto-restores after voting. Space is created and reclaimed dynamically. Nothing is static.
Desktop-only glow. The endgame glow at 95%+ purity — electric cyan and green, 240px radius, 4-5 shadow layers — looked mesmerising on a laptop. On a Samsung S23 it was choppy. On an S10 it was unplayable. So we auto-detect mobile and disable it entirely. Performance mode, stored in localStorage. No toggles. It just works.
Z-index war. The game has overlays on overlays — emergency screens, wheel spins, litigation iframes, advisor panels, debug tools, notifications. Getting the stacking order right across all of them took an entire z-index scale refactor. One of the 27 bugs was literally "Overlapping Overlay Z-Index Chaos."

Part 2: a player that renders your exact design over the actual video and lets you download an MP4. One SRT file with 48 subtitle entries. One afternoon. Five bugs that nearly killed us.

Word-by-word — the breakthrough.

First version displayed subtitle lines — entire sentences appearing at once. Dan tested it and said the method that worked best was each word independently, one after another, in the same spot. So we split every SRT entry by spaces. Each word gets an equal slice of the subtitle's duration. Font size scales by word length — short words go big (38px), long words go small (22px). Chorus words glow pink and red. Action words like "smash" and "stomp" shake orange. Six effects cycling: scale, fadeIn, scale, bounce, fadeIn, scale. Dead centre of the phone canvas, one word at a time.

The invisible words bug.

After getting word-by-word working, Dan reported that only the word "He" was visible. Every other word was invisible. Here's what happened: the render function was called every animation frame (60fps). Each frame, it destroyed all word elements and recreated them from scratch. The CSS animations — fadeIn, scale — start at opacity:0 and transition to opacity:1 with animation-fill-mode: forwards. But recreation every 16 milliseconds meant animations restarted at opacity:0 every single frame. They never got a chance to complete. Words were permanently invisible.

"He" was visible because it had the bounce animation — and bounce doesn't touch opacity. The fix: element reuse. Create each word element once when it first appears, reuse it on subsequent frames, only remove it when the word goes off-screen. Animation plays once, reaches opacity:1, and stays there. Subtle bug. Brutal to diagnose. Obvious in hindsight.

The centering war.

Before the invisible bug, words were visible but positioned off to the right. Dan could see "I like him" barely on the page. Root cause: CSS animations were overwriting the element's transform. Every word uses transform: translate(-50%,-50%) for centering. But the bounce keyframe set transform: translateY(0) — which completely replaced the centering transform. Every animated word lost its centering and snapped to the left edge. Fix: bake translate(-50%,-50%) into every single keyframe of every single animation. Bounce, shake, fadeInUp — all of them.

PART 1 / PART 2 — the splash system.

Dan wanted users to understand this was a two-step process. So we built massive splash screens — "PART 1" and "PART 2" in absurdly large font (14rem) that fill the page on load. A green pill button says "Go to Part 2 →" or "← Back to Part 1." Auto-retracts after 4 seconds into a subtle tab on the left edge. Hover the tab and it expands. Click it and you navigate. The editor saves all word data to localStorage before jumping — so the player receives the exact positions, colours, animations, and timing. Same design, seamlessly connected.
