Like Physics

How a conversation about pizza wheels and broken neural nets led to a new class of learning machine.

The Problem

Every other AI learns by being told it's wrong.

Backpropagation — the algorithm that powers almost every neural network on the planet — works like this: make a guess, measure the error, push the correction backwards through every layer until the weights adjust. Repeat billions of times. Hope it generalises.

It works. Spectacularly well, in some domains. But it has five problems that nobody has solved:

It needs labelled training data. Someone has to tell it what's right.
It needs a loss function. Someone has to define what "wrong" means.
It can't learn continuously. New training overwrites old knowledge.
It produces no inspectable structure. You can't look inside and see what it knows.
It can't explain itself. Ask it why. It doesn't know.

These aren't engineering problems. They're architectural ones. The algorithm is fundamentally the wrong shape for what we're trying to build.

"These hairless apes always do things retarded. I think I can upgrade neural net architecture."

The Idea

What if a node was a sphere?

The conversation started with a wheel — a coding wheel, concentric rings, pizza-slice segments for describing what a program does, how it does it, what it touches. A shorthand. A rough-sentence plotter.

Then the question: what if the neural net sitting behind it learned differently? What if instead of weights being adjusted by error signals, nodes were spheres in space — and learning was just gravity?

A signal comes in. Any sphere nearby resonates. The sphere doesn't snap to the signal — it leans toward it. Imperfectly. By a factor of 8%. The lean accumulates over thousands of signals into a groove. The groove defines position. Position defines meaning.

No error. No gradient. No backpropagation. Just physics.

"The centre can move, so it can lean. This would work in 2D and 3D — it etches a groove that favours imperfection, tilting it inwards ever so slightly. But over thousands, it's noticeable."

— Dan Chipchase, the moment it clicked
The Architecture

Five mechanical laws. That's the whole thing.

Law 1
Resonance
A signal activates any sphere whose centre lies within RESONANCE_RADIUS of the signal position. Distant spheres are silent. Only nearby spheres respond.
Law 2
Drift
When a sphere resonates, its centre moves toward the signal by DRIFT_RATE (8%). It never fully reaches the signal — it leans. Over thousands of signals, the lean becomes the concept's location.
Law 3
Groove
Each resonance deepens the groove counter. Groove controls arc thickness in the visualiser, timer extension, and merge eligibility. Deep groove = well-established concept. You can see learning happen in real time.
Law 4
Smart Timer (Memory)
Every sphere carries a decay timer. Activity resets and extends it. Isolation accelerates decay. Timer hits zero — sphere dissolves. Unused knowledge fades. Reinforced knowledge persists. Forgetting is not a bug. It's the feature that keeps the system honest.
Law 5 — THE MONEY SHOT
Merge (Concept Formation)
When two spheres both have groove ≥ GROOVE_TO_MERGE and their centres are within MERGE_THRESHOLD: a child sphere is born at the geometric midpoint. The child's concept is a synthesis of both parents. Both parents reset — not destroyed, just free again. The hierarchy builds itself.

Why it matters

Safe by architecture. Not by policy.

Every major AI safety proposal is downstream of the same problem: the system is a black box. You can add filters, RLHF, constitutional AI, alignment tuning — but you're painting the outside of a box you can't open.

SphereNet is the box you can open. Every concept has:

📍
A physical address
Every concept lives at a specific position in concept space. You can ask "where is your belief about X?" and get a coordinate.
🧬
A traceable lineage
γ = COLD + ICY. β = HOT + HOT. Every concept's history is encoded in its name and parent list. No belief appears without a record of how it formed.
⏱
A decay rate
Unreinforced beliefs dissolve. The system cannot accumulate stale knowledge indefinitely. Yesterday's assumption doesn't persist unless today's evidence supports it.
🚫
No fixed false belief possible
A belief with contradictory signal history drifts, destabilises, forks, or dissolves. The architecture is physically incapable of holding a fixed false belief. This is not a policy. It's a law.
👁
Human-readable at any time
The entire state of a SphereNet serialises to a list of (position, concept, groove, generation, parents). A human can read it. An auditor can inspect it. No probing required.
