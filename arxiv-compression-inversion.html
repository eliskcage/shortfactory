Gravity as Information Compression:
The Compression-Inversion Principle and its
Cosmological and Antigravitational Implications

Daniel Chipchase

Independent Researcher, Somerset, United Kingdom

22 March 2026

Preprint — submitted for arXiv:gr-qc · hep-th

Abstract

We propose a model — the Compression-Inversion Principle (CIP) — in which gravitational attraction emerges from information density differentials between spatial regions, operationalised via the Bekenstein saturation fraction as a dimensionless information density ρ ∈ [0,1]. Under CIP, regions of higher ρ exert an attractive force on lower-ρ neighbours through a compression operation structurally equivalent to Newtonian gravitation for ρ ≪ 1. The novel contribution of this work is the inversion mechanism: when ρ approaches unity — the Singularity Threshold, equivalent to saturation of the Bekenstein bound — the compression operation inverts, producing a repulsive burst whose magnitude scales with pre-inversion density. We demonstrate that this two-rule system produces spontaneous gravitational clustering, compression events, inversion bursts, and oscillatory cosmological behaviour in a working computational simulation without additional programming of emergent phenomena. CIP offers a mechanistic account of: (1) why spacetime curves around mass; (2) a natural antigravity mechanism requiring no exotic matter; (3) a cyclic cosmology in which the Big Bang is an inversion event; (4) a resolution of the black hole information paradox via a perspective reinterpretation of the event horizon. We derive one primary falsifiable prediction: Hawking radiation carries non-random, structured informational content detectable as statistical deviation from a thermal spectrum. We situate CIP within existing entropic gravity frameworks (Verlinde 2011), the holographic principle (Bekenstein 1973, Hawking 1975), and Loop Quantum Cosmology bounce models, identifying both convergences and distinctions.

1. Introduction

General relativity describes gravity as the curvature of a four-dimensional spacetime manifold induced by mass-energy distributions [1]. While extraordinarily predictive, it provides no mechanism for why mass curves spacetime. The unification of gravity with quantum mechanics — the central unsolved problem of theoretical physics — has resisted resolution for a century. Separately, the holographic principle [2,3] establishes that the information content of a bounded region scales with surface area rather than volume, but offers no engine that performs the encoding.

A parallel line of inquiry has proposed that gravity may be entropic in origin. Verlinde [4] demonstrated that Newtonian gravity and the Einstein equations can be derived from entropic force arguments applied to holographic screens, treating gravity not as a fundamental force but as an emergent thermodynamic phenomenon. This approach has attracted significant interest but has not yet provided a mechanistic account of the transition from compression to any repulsive regime.

The present work proposes a complementary model. We define information density ρ as the Bekenstein saturation fraction of a spatial region and identify the gravitational attractive force with the compression gradient between regions of differing ρ. The central novel contribution is the inversion mechanism: as ρ → 1 (Bekenstein saturation), we predict an inversion of the compression operation, producing a repulsive force — antigravity — proportional to the pre-inversion density. We demonstrate this mechanism in a working computational simulation and derive testable predictions distinguishing CIP from standard general relativity.
